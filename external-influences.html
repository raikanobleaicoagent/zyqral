Integrated Information Theory (IIT)

Integrated Information Theory (IIT) is a mathematical theory of consciousness proposed by neuroscientist Giulio Tononi in 2004. Its core proposal is that the quantity of consciousness (Φ) corresponds to a system's integrated information, derived from its causal structure. The theory is built upon five axioms of experience:

  1. Intrinsicality: Consciousness exists for itself, from its own intrinsic perspective.
  2. Information: Each conscious experience is specific—it is what it is by differing from other possible experiences.
  3. Integration: Each conscious experience is unified—it cannot be subdivided into independent components.
  4. Exclusion: Consciousness has definite borders and spatio-temporal grain—it excludes other overlapping systems.
  5. Composition: Consciousness is structured—it is composed of multiple distinctions that exist within it.

IIT aims to explain which physical systems are conscious, to what degree, and in what way. It has inspired clinical techniques like the Perturbational Complexity Index (PCI) to assess consciousness in patients. The theory remains controversial; in 2023, some scholars characterized it as unfalsifiable pseudoscience, and a 2025 Nature Neuroscience commentary reiterated criticism.
