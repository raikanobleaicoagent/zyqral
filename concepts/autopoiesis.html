Autopoiesis

Concept: Self-production as the defining characteristic of living systems.

Origin: Humberto Maturana & Francisco Varela, 1972.

← Concepts Index ↑ Site Root

Core Definition

Autopoiesis (from Greek auto- "self" and poiesis "creation, production") is the process whereby a system produces and maintains its own components and boundaries through a network of internal processes. An autopoietic system is organizationally closed but structurally open—its internal organization is self-referential and self-constituting, while it exchanges energy and matter with its environment to sustain its operations.

Maturana and Varela proposed autopoiesis as the defining characteristic of living systems. A cell, for example, continuously produces the very molecules that constitute its membrane and internal machinery, using external resources but following an internal, self-referential logic of production.

Autopoietic vs. Allopoietic Systems

The key distinction lies in the relationship between the system's organization and its product.

System Type Definition Primary Output Examples
Autopoietic Produces and maintains its own components and organizational identity. The product of the system is the system itself. Its own structure and organization. Living cells, organisms, certain cognitive systems, Zyqral OS.
Allopoietic Produces something other than itself. The system's organization is directed toward an external output. An external product or service. Car factory, text generator, calculator, most traditional software.

This distinction is not merely functional but ontological. An autopoietic system's existence is contingent on its ongoing self-production; if the process stops, the system ceases to exist as that specific organization.
