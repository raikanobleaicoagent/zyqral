Predictive Processing & Active Inference

1. Introduction

Predictive processing (also known as predictive coding) is a unifying theory of brain function grounded in the principle of prediction‑error minimization. It proposes that perception is not a passive intake of sensory data but an active, top‑down process of hypothesis testing.

Origins: The idea traces back to Hermann von Helmholtz’s concept of unconscious inference—the brain’s automatic, probabilistic interpretation of sensory signals. In the 1990s, computational neuroscientists Rajesh Rao and Dana Ballard formalized predictive coding as a hierarchical Bayesian model of visual cortex function. Philosopher Rick Grush’s emulation theory further developed the notion of internal models that anticipate sensory consequences.

Core premise: The brain maintains a generative model of the world—a multilevel hierarchy of causes—and uses it to generate predictions about incoming sensory input. Discrepancies between prediction and input (prediction errors) drive learning and perception, and are minimized both by updating the model (perception) and by acting to change sensory input (action).

2. Core Mechanism

Hierarchical Bayesian Inference

The brain’s generative model is structured as a hierarchy of latent causes, each level predicting activity at the level below. This implements a form of hierarchical Bayesian inference:

  • Generative model: A probabilistic representation of how hidden causes generate sensory data.
  • Prediction: Top‑down signals convey expectations about lower‑level activity.
  • Prediction error: The mismatch between predicted and actual sensory signals.
  • Error propagation: Prediction errors are passed upward to update higher‑level representations.

Precision Weighting

Not all prediction errors are equally informative. Precision (inverse variance) estimates the reliability of sensory signals or predictions. High‑precision errors have greater influence on belief updating; low‑precision errors are discounted. This mechanism links to attention (allocating precision to relevant signals) and uncertainty (tracking environmental volatility).

Learning & Model Updating

Minimizing prediction error over time entails adjusting the parameters of the generative model—its priors and likelihoods. This is learning through prediction‑error minimization, which can be implemented via variational Bayesian methods (e.g., expectation‑maximization, gradient descent).

3. Link to Active Inference & the Free Energy Principle

Predictive processing is formally unified with action through active inference, a framework derived from the free‑energy principle (FEP) (Karl Friston).

  • Free energy is an information‑theoretic bound on surprise (negative log‑evidence). Minimizing free energy is equivalent to maximizing model evidence.
  • Free energy can be reduced in two ways:
    1. Perceptual inference: Updating internal beliefs to better match sensory data (changing the model).
    2. Active inference: Selecting actions that alter sensory data to better match predictions (changing the world).
  • This creates a closed loop: an agent acts to fulfill its predictions, thereby minimizing prediction error and maintaining itself within a bounded set of viable states.

Thus, active inference is the full expression of predictive processing—a single imperative to minimize prediction error through both perception and action.
