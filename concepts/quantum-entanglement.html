Quantum Entanglement

A foundational phenomenon in quantum mechanics where the quantum states of two or more particles become interdependent, such that the state of one cannot be described independently of the state of the others, even when separated by large distances.

Definition & Core Principle

Quantum entanglement describes a strong correlation that exists between quantum systems. When particles (e.g., photons, electrons) interact or are generated in a way that their total quantum state is inseparable, they become "entangled." Measuring a property (like spin or polarization) of one particle instantaneously determines the corresponding property of its entangled partner, regardless of the spatial distance separating them. This correlation violates classical intuitions of locality and realism, suggesting a form of non-local connection intrinsic to quantum theory.

The entangled state is described by a single quantum wavefunction for the composite system. This wavefunction does not factor into independent wavefunctions for each particle, a property known as non-separability.

Historical Context: The EPR Paradox

The concept gained prominence through the 1935 paper by Einstein, Podolsky, and Rosen (EPR). They used a thought experiment involving entangled particles to argue that quantum mechanics was an incomplete theory. If measuring one particle instantly affects another far away (what Einstein called "spooky action at a distance"), it would violate the principle of locality (no faster-than-light influence). EPR concluded that there must be "hidden variables" not described by quantum mechanics that determine particle properties pre-measurement.

Einstein, A., Podolsky, B., & Rosen, N. (1935). Can Quantum-Mechanical Description of Physical Reality Be Considered Complete? Physical Review, 47(10), 777–780.

Experimental Verification & Bell's Theorem

In 1964, physicist John S. Bell formulated a theorem (Bell's inequalities) that provided a testable criterion to distinguish between local hidden variable theories and quantum mechanics. If experiments violated Bell's inequalities, local realism would be falsified, confirming the non-local correlations predicted by quantum entanglement.

Key Experiments:

  • Alain Aspect (1980s): Performed a series of experiments with entangled photons that demonstrated clear violations of Bell's inequalities under conditions that closed major "loopholes."
  • Subsequent Experiments (1990s–2010s): Groups worldwide (Zeilinger, Wineland, Haroche) conducted experiments with greater distances and efficiency, consistently confirming quantum non-locality.
  • 2022 Nobel Prize in Physics: Awarded to Alain Aspect, John F. Clauser, and Anton Zeilinger "for experiments with entangled photons, establishing the violation of Bell inequalities and pioneering quantum information science." This prize cemented entanglement as an empirically verified physical reality and the cornerstone of quantum information technologies.

Key Applications

Entanglement is not merely a curiosity; it is a resource for new technologies.

Quantum Computing

Entangled qubits enable quantum parallelism and algorithms (e.g., Shor's factoring, Grover's search) that outperform classical counterparts for specific problems. Entanglement is essential for quantum error correction and fault-tolerant computation.

Quantum Cryptography (QKD)

Quantum Key Distribution (e.g., BB84, E91 protocols) uses entangled photon pairs to generate secure cryptographic keys. Any eavesdropping attempt disturbs the entangled state, alerting the communicating parties—a principle based on the no-cloning theorem.

Quantum Teleportation

A protocol to transfer the quantum state of a particle to a distant location using a pair of entangled particles and classical communication. It demonstrates the non-local transfer of quantum information.

Quantum Networks & Sensors

Entanglement can enhance the precision of measurements (quantum metrology) and enable the linking of quantum processors into a future quantum internet.
