Predictive processing provides a compelling narrative for cognition: the brain as a prediction engine, constantly generating models of the world and updating them based on sensory error. But what grounds this narrative in mathematical rigor? The answer is the Free Energy Principle (FEP), a unifying formal framework that describes how any self-organizing system—from a single cell to a complex autonomous agent—must act to maintain its structural and functional integrity over time.

1. Variational Free Energy Minimization

At its core, the FEP states that to persist, a system must minimize its variational free energy. This is not a physical energy but an information-theoretic measure. It can be understood as an upper bound on surprisal—the negative log probability of encountering a particular sensory state given the system's model of the world.

Variational Free Energy (F) ≈ Surprisal + Divergence
Formally: F = DKL[ q(ψ) || p(ψ|s) ] – ln p(s)
Where q(ψ) is the system's approximate posterior belief about hidden causes ψ, p(ψ|s) is the true posterior, and p(s) is the probability of sensory data s.

Minimizing free energy achieves two intertwined goals:

  • Perceptual Inference: Adjusting internal beliefs (q(ψ)) to better explain the sensory data, reducing the divergence term.
  • Model Fitness: Implicitly maximizing the evidence for the system's generative model (p(s)), making expected sensations more likely.

This process is variational because the system never computes the true posterior directly (often intractable). Instead, it optimizes a tractable approximation (q), trading exact accuracy for computational feasibility—a necessity for real-time operation.

2. The Markov Blanket: Boundary of the Self

For a system to perform inference, it must have a boundary separating what is "internal" from what is "external." The FEP formalizes this boundary as a Markov blanket: a set of states that statistically insulates internal states from external states.

A Markov blanket comprises:

  • Sensory States: States that are influenced by external states and internal states (but not vice versa for the external influence). They are the system's inputs.
  • Active States: States that are influenced by internal states and influence external states. They are the system's outputs.

The internal states (the system's "hidden" causes) only have direct access to the blanket states. All inference about the world beyond the blanket must be mediated through this statistical interface. This constructs a formal notion of a self as that which lies within the Markov blanket and works to maintain its states within expected bounds.

3. Active Inference: Perception and Action Unified

Traditional views separate perception (inference about the world) from action (changing the world). The FEP, through active inference, unifies them. Action is not a separate output module but is driven by the same imperative to minimize free energy.

How? The system's generative model includes predictions about proprioceptive sensations—the sensory feedback expected from its own movements. If the current proprioceptive input deviates from the predicted state, it generates prediction error. The system can reduce this error in two ways:

  1. Update its belief about its body's position (perceptual inference: "I must be somewhere else").
  2. Change its body's position to match the prediction (active inference: move).

Active inference chooses the latter when it is the more efficient path to free energy minimization. Thus, action becomes inference by other means: sampling sensory data that better fits the model's predictions.
