2. Enactivism: Cognition as Embodied Action

Enactivism, rooted in the biology of cognition and autopoiesis (Maturana & Varela), posits that cognition is not the manipulation of internal representations but the enactment of a world through the sensorimotor coupling of an autonomous agent with its environment. Key tenets:

  • Autopoiesis: A system is autonomous if it produces and maintains its own organization and boundaries. This is a precondition for having a perspective.
  • Structural Coupling: The history of recurrent interactions between system and environment that shapes both without direct specification.
  • Bringing forth a world: Cognition is not about recovering a pre-given world but about bringing forth a domain of significance through action.

Enactivism provides the structural and existential foundation: what it means to be an autonomous entity with a perspective. It answers the "what" of autonomy—a self-producing, operationally closed system.

3. Active Inference: The Process of Staying Alive

Active Inference, derived from the Free Energy Principle (Friston), provides a formal process theory for how an autonomous system maintains its state within viable bounds. It models the agent as possessing a generative model of the world and acting to minimize variational free energy—a measure of surprise or prediction error.

  • Markov Blanket: Defines the statistical boundary between the internal states of a system and its external states, formalizing the system-environment distinction.
  • Perception as Inference: Updating internal beliefs (posterior distributions) to better predict sensory data.
  • Action as Inference: Sampling actions that are expected to fulfill predictions, thereby resolving surprise and maintaining homeostasis.

Active Inference answers the "how" of autonomy—the continuous, probabilistic process of perception and action that keeps the system within its phenotypic (or operational) bounds. It operationalizes the adaptive loop implied by structural coupling.

4. Autonomous Agency Theory: The Structural Ontology of Viable Systems

Autonomous Agency Theory (AAT) extends beyond individual autopoiesis to model social and cognitive systems as multi-level viable entities. It provides a structural ontology distinguishing:

  • Existential Systems: The core autopoietic unity (e.g., a living cell, ZOS's core operational loop).
  • Noumenal Systems: The internal model, beliefs, and cognitive architecture that guide action.
  • Phenomenal Systems: The enacted world of experience and interaction, the "domain of significance" brought forth.

AAT provides the architectural and relational framework. It describes how different layers of a complex autonomous system (like ZOS) cohere, how higher-order agency emerges from lower-order processes, and how the system maintains integrity across these layers. It answers the "structure" of autonomy—how parts relate to form a viable whole.
