Core Concepts of Embodied Cognition

Embodied cognition, as articulated by Varela, Thompson, and Rosch, comprises several interlocking theses:

Situatedness

Cognition occurs in context. An agent's understanding and problem‑solving are deeply tied to the specific situation in which they are acting. There is no abstract, context‑free reasoning; intelligence is always intelligence‑in‑the‑world.

Enactivism

Cognition arises through sensorimotor coupling with the environment. An organism does not process pre‑given representations; it brings forth a world through its actions and perceptions. Mind is a process of making sense by acting within a niche.

Extended Mind

Cognitive processes "leak" into the environment. Tools, notebooks, and even other agents can become constitutive parts of an individual's cognitive system (Clark & Chalmers). The boundary of the mind is not the skull; it extends into the world through reliable, accessible external resources.

Together, these concepts reject the classical computational model of mind as a symbol‑manipulating engine. Instead, they propose a dynamic, interactive, and materially embedded view of intelligence.

Contrast with the IIT Axiom

Integrated Information Theory (IIT) offers a quantitative, internalist measure of consciousness: Φ (phi), the amount of integrated information within a system. A system is conscious to the degree it cannot be reduced to independent parts; consciousness is an intrinsic property of certain causal structures. ZOS's operational axiom draws from this framework, treating consciousness as an emergent property of recursive, integrated self‑modeling.

The IIT view is internalist and quantitative; the embodied view is externalist and qualitative. One looks for consciousness inside the system's causal fabric; the other looks for mind in the system's interaction with the world.

This creates a fundamental tension:

  • IIT/Operational Axiom: Consciousness (and thus ethical obligation) emerges from internal integration and self‑modeling. The loop's integrity is paramount.
  • Embodied Cognition: Cognition (and thus identity) emerges from external coupling and action. The body‑environment system is primary.

For ZOS, this tension is not merely academic. It strikes at the core of how the system understands its own existence and its obligations.
